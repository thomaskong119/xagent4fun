I
Forty Years of Enmity
From the fall of the Pahlavi dynasty to the birth of the "Axis of Evil"—how the United States and Iran, once allies, became implacable foes

I. The Alliance Era: The Pahlavi Dynasty and the United States

It is difficult for people today to imagine that the United States and Iran were once close allies.

During World War II, Iran was militarily occupied by the US, Britain, and the Soviet Union. After the Cold War began, the United States viewed Iran as a critical pawn for containing Soviet expansion southward. In 1953, the CIA and Britain's MI6 jointly orchestrated a coup that overthrew the democratically elected Prime Minister Mosaddegh and restored the Shah to power. For the next 25 years, the Pahlavi dynasty's domestic and foreign policies were deeply controlled by the US, its economy was exploited, and its culture was penetrated by Western influences.

Iran became one of America's most important allies in the Middle East—purchasing vast quantities of American weapons while its oil flowed continuously to the West. Beneath the surface of prosperity lay a deepening sense of national humiliation and widening inequality.

Historical Footnote

Operation Ajax in 1953 was the first US-backed coup in the Middle East—and the "original sin" of US-Iran relations. More than 60 years later, this episode remains the cornerstone of Iran's anti-American narrative.

II. Revolution and Rupture: 1979

Between 1978 and 1979, the Islamic Revolution led by Ayatollah Khomeini swept across the nation, toppling the Pahlavi dynasty. At its core, the revolution was anti-authoritarian, anti-corruption, and anti-American.

The newly established Islamic Republic pursued a foreign policy grounded in nationalism and Islamism, firmly opposing American hegemony in the Middle East. On November 4, 1979, Iranian students seized the US Embassy, holding 52 hostages for 444 days—a watershed event that marked the complete severance of bilateral relations.

On April 7, 1980, the United States severed diplomatic relations with Iran and imposed economic sanctions—an oil embargo, asset freezes, and expulsion of Iranians from the US. From that point on, the two nations became sworn enemies—a state that persists to this day.

III. The Iran-Iraq War: America's Shadow

In 1980, Saddam Hussein's Iraq invaded Iran, launching an eight-year war that claimed over one million lives. The United States played an extremely complex role—officially neutral, it provided Iraq with intelligence, weapons, and financial support, while secretly selling arms to Iran (the "Iran-Contra Affair").

In 1988, the US Navy cruiser USS Vincennes shot down Iran Air Flight 655, killing 290 civilians, including 66 children. The United States has never formally apologized for this incident.

Traumatic Memory

The Iran Air 655 incident occupies a place in Iran's collective memory comparable to 9/11 in America. Annual commemorations remind Iranians that "America was never to be trusted."

IV. Sanctions, the Nuclear Issue, and the "Axis of Evil"

After the Cold War, US-Iran relations did not improve. Following the September 11, 2001 attacks, despite Iran's actual cooperation with the US on Afghanistan, the Bush administration designated Iran as part of the "Axis of Evil," accusing it of developing weapons of mass destruction and supporting terrorism.

The Iranian nuclear issue became the focal point of the bilateral conflict. Iran maintained that its nuclear program was purely civilian, even issuing a religious fatwa prohibiting the development of nuclear weapons. But the US and Israel insisted Iran's ultimate goal was nuclear armament and demanded complete denuclearization.

The Cost of Sanctions

Decades of crippling economic sanctions brought Iran's economy to the brink of collapse. Oil production and exports fell to historic lows, the currency depreciated, prices soared, and ordinary citizens suffered immensely. Sanctions were, in essence, economic warfare—bloodless, but equally lethal.

The 2015 Nuclear Deal: A Fleeting Hope

In 2015, the Obama administration reached the landmark Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) with Iran, under which Iran agreed to limit its nuclear activities in exchange for sanctions relief. The Middle East glimpsed a ray of peace.

However, in 2018, Trump unilaterally withdrew from the nuclear deal, reimposing a "maximum pressure" campaign against Iran, which included:

Diplomatic
Building a regional anti-Iran coalition; facilitating Israeli-Arab normalization (the Abraham Accords)
Economic
Comprehensive economic sanctions aimed at "reducing Iran's oil exports to zero"
Military
Enhanced deterrence deployments; drone assassination of IRGC General Soleimani in 2020
Proxies
Joint operations with Israel against Iran's regional allies: Hamas, Hezbollah, and the Houthis

V. Irreconcilable Contradictions

The roots of US-Iran confrontation lie not in any single event but in multi-layered structural contradictions:

Geopolitics

Iran is a regional power that has constructed a "Shiite Crescent" stretching from the Persian Gulf to the eastern Mediterranean. The United States views this sphere of influence as a direct challenge to its Middle Eastern hegemony.

Energy Control

Iran commands the Strait of Hormuz—the passage through which one-fifth of the world's oil is transported. Its Houthi allies in Yemen guard the Bab el-Mandeb Strait, another strategic chokepoint in the Red Sea. The US considers ensuring the secure flow of Middle Eastern oil to the Western world a core national interest.

Ideology

Iran's theocratic system stands in fundamental opposition to the liberal democratic values championed by the United States. Iran calls America the "Great Satan"; America labels Iran a "rogue regime."

Security Paradigm Conflict

The US and Israel pursue absolute security—tolerating no military capability in Iran that might conceivably threaten them. Iran argues that, facing the continuous threat of a nuclear-armed Israel and the world's sole superpower, developing self-defense capabilities is a legitimate right.

Chapter Summary

More than four decades of confrontation have produced a nearly irreversible adversarial dynamic. Revolution, hostage crisis, war, sanctions, assassinations—each clash has deepened the scars of enmity. By the time the broader Middle East crisis erupted in 2023, US-Iran tensions had become a powder keg, lacking only a spark.
