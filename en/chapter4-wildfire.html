I. Multi-Theater Situation

From day one, the US-Iran war was never a conflict between just two nations. It engulfed the entire Middle East and began spreading globally. By the end of March, at least eight countries and territories had been directly struck or drawn into the fighting.

🔥 Iranian Homeland
Primary Theater
Sustained US-Israeli airstrikes targeting military, political, and economic infrastructure. 153 cities across all 31 provinces hit. Iran's air defense network severely degraded.
🔥 Strait of Hormuz
Oil Chokepoint
Iran attacked commercial tankers in an attempt to blockade the strait. One-fifth of global oil shipments disrupted, triggering the largest energy supply disruption in history.
🔥 Israel
Under Fire from Three Directions
Iranian missiles + Hezbollah rockets + Houthi ballistic missiles — Israel faced simultaneous attacks from three fronts.
⚡ Lebanon
Second Front
Hezbollah entered the war in full force. Israel's ground incursion advanced to the Litani River, announcing plans to destroy buildings in southern Lebanon.
⚡ Gulf States
Collateral Zone
Iran struck US military facilities and allied targets in or near Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, and Oman.
🌊 Indian Ocean
Extended Maritime Theater
US submarine sank an Iranian frigate off Sri Lanka; Iranian missiles reportedly struck the US base at Diego Garcia (2,500 miles from Iran).

III. The Strait of Hormuz: Choking the World's Lifeline

Among all theaters, the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz produced the most far-reaching global consequences.

This waterway, at its narrowest just 33 kilometers wide, carries approximately one-fifth of the world's oil shipments and a substantial volume of liquefied natural gas trade. From the first day of war, Iran began attacking commercial vessels transiting the strait, attempting to sever this lifeline of the global economy.

IEA: Supply Disruption

The International Energy Agency (IEA) characterized this disruption as "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." Multiple analytical firms warned that a prolonged blockade would "guarantee a global recession."

A Replay of the 1970s?

Many economists have drawn parallels between the current crisis and the oil crises of the 1970s. But the scale may be far larger—the 1973 Arab oil embargo affected exports from select producing nations, while the Strait of Hormuz blockade directly disrupts the main artery of global oil transportation.

IV. The Kurdish Card and Regime Change Ambitions

Beyond military strikes, the US and Israel also actively pursued the internal fragmentation of Iran:

The Kurdish factor — The US and Israel openly supported Kurdish rebel forces in military operations against Iran, establishing an aerial strike corridor in Iran's Kurdistan region. The aim was to foment civil war and engineer ethnic division.

"Color revolution" — While striking Iran's public security forces from the air, attempts were made to incite "street revolutions" among the populace, seeking to dismantle the regime from within.

Information warfare — Spreading rumors of divisions within Iran's leadership, attempting to drive a wedge between the IRGC and the regular military, and inciting military mutiny.

A Multi-Pronged Strategy

The US-Israeli war against Iran was not merely a military operation but a comprehensive playbook of "military strikes + regime change + economic strangulation + information warfare." The objective extended beyond destroying Iran's military capability—it aimed to fundamentally reshape Iran's political regime and the regional order.

V. The Shadow of Ground Invasion

By late March, an even more alarming signal emerged: US ground troops began arriving in the Middle East in significant numbers.

On March 28, 3,500 Marines aboard the amphibious assault ship USS Tripoli arrived in the Gulf region. Another 1,500 soldiers from the 82nd Airborne Division were en route. A New York Times analysis bore the blunt headline: "Trump Faces Decision on Whether to Launch a Ground War in Iran."

From airstrikes to ground invasion—should this step be taken, the war would escalate to an entirely different level. With a population of nearly 90 million and complex terrain, a ground war could prove far costlier than the Iraq War.

Chapter Summary

The wildfire has spread far beyond the borders of the US and Iran. Hezbollah, the Houthis, Gulf states, the Indian Ocean—each new theater increases the complexity of the conflict and the risk of losing control. The Strait of Hormuz blockade has transmitted the war's impact to every gas station and grocery store on the planet. And the arrival of ground troops suggests the worst-case scenario may yet lie ahead.
