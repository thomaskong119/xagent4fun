Operation Epic Fury

I. D-Day: February 28

In the early hours of February 28, 2026, having completed its Middle East military deployment, the United States launched a massive joint military strike with Israel against Iran, codenamed "Operation Epic Fury." It was the largest US military operation in the Middle East since the 2003 Iraq War.

II. Iran Strikes Back

Despite the elimination of its top leadership in a single blow, Iranian military forces launched large-scale retaliation within hours—demonstrating a degree of distributed resilience within its command structure.

Iran's retaliatory targets included:

  • Israeli homeland — sustained ballistic missile and drone attacks
  • US bases in the Middle East — facilities in Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia
  • Strait of Hormuz — attacks on commercial oil tankers in an attempt to blockade the strait
  • Gulf states — Saudi Arabia, UAE, and others viewed as US allies

III. The First Week: A Staggering 72 Hours

March 1 — First US Military Deaths

An Iranian drone struck the US military base at Shuaiba Port in Kuwait, killing 6 American soldiers—the first US military fatalities of the conflict. On the same day, three US F-15 fighter jets were downed by Kuwaiti air defense systems in a friendly fire incident.

Also on this day, the first commercial oil tankers were struck by missiles in the Strait of Hormuz, signaling Iran's formal initiation of its strategy to blockade the world's most critical oil transit route.

March 2 — Hezbollah Opens a Second Front

Lebanese Hezbollah fired rockets into northern Israel, claiming retaliation for months of sustained Israeli airstrikes on Lebanon. Israel immediately intensified its strikes on Lebanon, including a new round of Beirut airstrikes and ground incursion operations.

March 4 — Iranian Frigate Sunk

A US submarine sank the Iranian naval frigate "Dena" off the coast of Sri Lanka, killing at least 104 crew members. The battlefield had expanded from the Persian Gulf to the Indian Ocean.

On the same day, Israel issued an "urgent evacuation warning" to all residents south of the Litani River in southern Lebanon.

March 7 — Environmental Catastrophe

Israel struck multiple oil storage facilities in Tehran and other locations, releasing large quantities of toxic substances into urban areas. Iran characterized the attack as "destroying the environment and seriously threatening lives," marking the conflict's entry into a "dangerous new phase."

First Week Damage Assessment

Iran: Supreme Leader + multiple core military-political leaders killed; 1,300+ dead, 5,000+ wounded (official 9-day figures); nuclear facilities, military bases, and civilian infrastructure extensively destroyed

United States: 6 soldiers killed in Kuwait; 3 F-15s lost to friendly fire; multiple Middle East military installations hit by missiles

Global: Commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz disrupted; oil prices surging sharply; airspace closures across multiple countries

IV. Birth of a New Leader

On March 8, just eight days after the Supreme Leader's assassination, Iran's Assembly of Experts elected Mojtaba Khamenei—the slain Supreme Leader's son—as the new Supreme Leader. His candidacy reportedly had the support of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps.

The speed of this power transition far exceeded outside expectations, sending a powerful signal: the US-Israeli strategic objective of achieving rapid regime change through "decapitation" had essentially failed.

Analysis

The swift selection of a new leader demonstrated the resilience of Iran's political system. The IRGC played a central stabilizing role during the crisis, serving as both the backbone of military strength and the guarantor of political succession. The US-Israeli script of "decapitation → chaos → regime collapse" did not materialize.

V. A War Planned at the Negotiating Table

Looking back at the context of the war's outbreak, one fact is inescapable:

This war was launched during active nuclear negotiations.

On February 6, both sides had just resumed nuclear talks in Muscat. On February 25, Iran's Foreign Minister publicly stated that a "historic deal was within reach." Three days later, bombs fell on Tehran.

An analysis published in Qiushi Journal noted: "The United States and Israel, without UN Security Council authorization, attacked and killed Iran's Supreme Leader, deliberately provoking a war against Iran. This is a war that should never have happened."

From the perspective of international law, launching a surprise attack during peace negotiations, waging war against a sovereign nation without UN Security Council authorization, and openly assassinating a head of state—these actions constitute grave violations of international law and the UN Charter.

Chapter Summary

The first week of Operation Epic Fury laid bare the most brutal dimensions of modern warfare: precision decapitation strikes, mass civilian casualties, environmental catastrophe, and simultaneous naval blockade. Iran lost its Supreme Leader and core military-political leadership but swiftly completed a power transition. US-Israeli expectations of a "swift victory" began unraveling from the very first week—Iran did not collapse; it chose total resistance.
